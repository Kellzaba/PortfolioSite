Design Dilemma

An unusual request came in at Corporate Executive Board for a custom page that broke the usual “template” for binder pages.

Reasoning & Process

Even though the layout was clearly going to break the standard mold, I wanted to make sure that the page still looked like it belonged in the binder, so I tweaked some existing building graphics and created some new ones to match in a coordinating style. There was a lot of text and images that needed to fit into this defined space, so my first idea was to place the text around the outside of the content area and have the graphic entirely on the inside of that. I highlighted buildings that the callouts were pointing to in blue to make them stand out from the busyness of the rest of the graphic. There is a key that helps to make sense of the different line types used to give additional information.

After feedback and deciding that the whole page should be done in grayscale, I recolored the graphics to light and dark gray to help distinguish which buildings were being called out. We also decided to make the graphic take up the whole content area, and layer the text right over the graphics. The line-type key was still used to provide more information graphically.

After more back-and-forth, the finalized design stayed black and white, though all shades were lightened so they page didn’t feel heavy. The image still took up the whole content area with text layered over, but we grouped the text back into one box for each callout, with specific pieces called out further by the use of our line-type key.

Solution

This page got a lot of feedback, and I worked closely with another designer at Corporate Executive Board to get it just right. Her feedback presented me with challenges, but it asked me to learn and grow while I made change after change to bring this page to a successful end point.

Client Feedback

“I wanted to take a minute of your time to thank you for your perseverance with page 8 in the Retail Executive Circle deck. If I am right, that is now a page that the 25 executives who run banking are going to photocopy, tack on their walls, and send to their teams. It is the culmination of 18 months of hard thinking that the group has done, it represents the biggest change in how retail banking works since the 1970s and the introduction of the ATM, and it is all captured in 8 by 11 inches. I know it was painful to keep iterating through the changes. But what you have produced is perfect and will be so helpful to members! Thank you!”

Client Feedback

"The meeting actually went spectacularly well. We got a 92% net promoter score. That is only the third or fourth time in CEB history that a meeting with over 10 members (and we had 27) has scored over a 90 and stands as a clear indication that the members were delighted with the meeting. The next time the group is getting together we will be in Toronto and our host has decided to invite the Prime Minister of Canada to join us for dinner. So something is working!

There were no challenges that I am aware of that your team could improve on. Cate may have some suggestions. As to successes, I am not sure if you ever had the chance to step back from all the urgent work and look at the binder. But it was a fascinating end product. It probably had the widest variety of content that we have ever had—member presentations, member artifacts, industry data, poll data, case studies, and insight pages. Your work managed to preserve the essence of the variety of inputs without allowing it to get the feel of scrap book. The end result was this wonderfully eclectic binder that felt like a gathering of all of the best stuff out there right now. At the end of the meeting I walked up to one member who ALWAYS has us ship his binder back for him to ask him if he wanted us to ship it. He literally held it up and looked at it for maybe four or five seconds, then pulled it to his chest and said, 'no, I don’t think I want this out of my sight.' That was a cool moment.

I know that as a firm we are trying to standardize to a branded image and that the Executive Circle program asks your team to do a lot of peculiar things instead. But if you had seen the almost affectionate manner in which Tim held his binder I know that you would have smiled, and I hope that you would have felt that all of the weirdness around the process was worth it."

[This mentioned binder, containing the “bank page,” was the product of the efforts of multiple people including me.]
