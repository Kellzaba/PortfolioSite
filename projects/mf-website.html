Design Dilemma

This project was an independent website redesign. Looking at the Mapleville Farm website, it seemed static and in need of an updated look. The home and about us pages lack a strong visual interest and are dominated by large areas of text, and all of the product pages are hard to read through: with each product listing taking up a full-horizontal row, to see the complete assortment of available goods requires a lot of scrolling. Additionally, the small product images seemed dwarfed in the long space. Having the price set in the same type as the description and only separated by a line-space, doesn’t seem ideal. There are also pages where products are missing or even the entire page doesn’t exist (yet.) Generally, the color scheme also seems a little drab for what should be a little more exciting—who doesn’t love homemade baked goods?

Reasoning & Process

As always, questions come first:

What kind of company is this?

Mapleville Farm is a small, family-operated retail bakery located in Rural Rhode Island.

What are the products like?

They sell a wide variety of food goods, from breads, to cookies, to pies, and more. The goods range from traditional to fun and interesting.

First, I worked out the bones of the new site on paper, and then created a mock-up in Photoshop. I sketched out a simple header for the top with a clear navigation bar for the main pages and sub-navigation bar for the product categories. For the home page, I thought a large image with a short text blurb would make a great spotlight display for a particular product that was special and could be rotated out based on season or otherwise. I developed a 2:1 framework for the main pages, and a three-column layout for the product pages.

Once I laid out my vision in Photoshop, I finalized more of the visual details: typefaces, sizes, colors, spacing, etc. I brightened the color scheme a little bit, without overdoing it, using subdued greens paired with a blue that makes reading the black body text a little easier on the eyes, and, for a little visual pop, a berry-wine color for titles.

I decided to only remake pages that had products, and I pulled actual content from their existing site (and facebook page.) I edited images for the product pages, and added new ones to the home and about pages to pair with the text. I decided to showcase the products in a circular image format to break up the rigidness of straight lines prominent in the navigation bars and in the obvious grid.

Solution

In addition to the reworked header, the new version of their site features a footer that contains relevant contact information, for easier access on any page in the site. The navigation bar has been split into four main pages and a sub-navigation bar that separates all of the product pages from the main navigation. Both the home page and the About Us page are now a little more readable, with the addition of images to break up the original monotony of text and with a more readable line-length. The product pages are easier to browse with three products to a row instead of one. The round images break up the hard edges, and the prices are much more prominent. As a bonus, I designed a custom favicon of a loaf of bread! Overall, there is now a cleaner design and easier, more convenient access to information for the user.
